Why I Teach

I teach because I love to learn, and I've discovered that teaching is the most powerful form of learning there is. Every time I step into the classroom, I'm not just sharing what I know; I'm wrestling with ideas alongside my students, finding new angles on texts I've read a hundred times, and thinking more carefully about language than I ever could alone.

My 9th graders remind me what it feels like to encounter a story for the very first time. My 12th graders push me to defend every claim with evidence. Together, we build something I couldn't build without them: a classroom where curiosity is the point, and where the question matters more than the answer.

"The best way to truly understand something is to have to teach it, so I never stop being a student."

CSTP Growth & Development

My Professional
Growth Journey

A reflective comparison of my Initial and Final CSTP Self-Assessment, tracing the arc of growth across a transformative year of teaching.

01

Where I Feel Strong

CSTP 6 · Developing as a Professional Educator

Professional Growth is where I feel the most solid, and honestly it makes sense. I have always been someone who reflects, asks hard questions, and comes back the next day ready to try something different. On top of that, creating a classroom where every student actually feels seen has been something I have been intentional about from day one. The culturally responsive piece is not a strategy I layer on top of my teaching. It is just how I teach. Most of my practice is sitting somewhere between Applying and Integrating, and I feel good about that foundation.

02

Where I Want to Grow

CSTP 5 · Assessing Students for Learning

Assessment is the big one. I am getting better at seeing what students can do, but I want to actually use that information to change what I do next, to let students into that process too, and to tell families more than just a grade. I also want to get better at making content genuinely accessible, not just rigorous on paper. And I want to stop waiting for professional growth to come to me. Seeking out feedback and connecting more with colleagues is something I need to be more deliberate about.

03

What I Am Most Proud Of

CSTP 4 · Planning Instruction and Designing Learning Experiences

The shift in how I plan instruction is the thing I feel most in my bones. I went from thinking broadly about what students need to actually designing around who they are, their backgrounds, their stories, their strengths. I am proud that my equity work has not been surface level. I have sat with uncomfortable things and let them actually change me. But more than any single thing, I am proud that I kept showing up. Not perfectly. Just consistently. That habit of returning to the work and asking how I can do better is something I built this year and I am not letting it go.

"Not perfection, but persistence. Returning to the student, returning to the question of how I can do better."

01 Teacher Wellness

Inquiry 1 · Sept – Oct 2025 · CSTP 6A

Can taking care of myself make me a better teacher?

I started this inquiry because I was overwhelmed. The planning, the grading, the emails were all piling up outside of class time and I was noticing it bleed into my teaching. So I committed to a daily 10-minute meditation using the Calm app and tracked my anxiety morning and night for the whole month of September.

By the end of the month my anxiety scores had dropped pretty consistently from the 6s and 7s I was logging in early September down to the 2s and 3s. That headspace made a real difference. I started using more Checks for Understanding during lessons instead of just clicking through slides. I found time to create modified assignments for SPED students that I honestly would have skipped when I was in survival mode. And when a stressful email hit right before class one day, I was actually able to breathe through it and not take it out on my students.

The big takeaway: a regulated teacher is a better teacher. When I was less anxious, my students got more of me, and a more intentional version of me at that.

Inquiry 2 · Oct – Dec 2025 · CSTP 4B

How do I actually make distance learning more engaging?

Online, it is easy to just click through slides while students zone out. I knew I was not using the tools available to me and I wanted to change that. I set out to learn and implement four or five new distance learning tools, each with a specific purpose, rather than just trying things randomly.

Not everything worked as planned. Listenwise was paywalled. Gameinar was too clunky. But that trial-and-error process was genuinely useful, because talking with colleagues about what did not work led me straight to what did: waterfall polls in the Zoom chat, Blooket for gamified review, and Zoom Polls for quick pulse checks. I also earned my Google Educator certification at both the Basic and Intermediate levels, which opened up new ways to use Forms and streamline parent communication.

As classrooms continue to evolve, the educators who take time to genuinely learn their tools, rather than just tolerate them, will be the ones who show up most fully for their students. This inquiry was a first step in becoming that kind of teacher.

Inquiry 3 · Jan – Mar 2026 · CSTP 5

What happens when students actually know what is expected?

Before this inquiry, my grading was pretty subjective. Students turned in work, I gave feedback based on what I was looking for, but that standard lived in my head and nowhere else. Almost everyone got a 10/10 on the American Born Chinese assignment regardless of how strong their response actually was, because without a rubric there was no real way to justify taking points off.

I introduced a C-E-R framework (Claim, Evidence, Reasoning) alongside a visible 5-point rubric for the Outliers assignment. The shift was immediate and concrete. Students wrote more. They pulled specific evidence from the text. They tried to build an actual argument. The grade distribution went from a flat row of 10s to a genuine spread that actually reflected quality and effort.

The line that stuck with me from reflecting on this: expectations moved from being in my head to being on the page. That one shift made grading faster, feedback more honest, and student work more intentional.

Inquiry 4 · Mar – Apr 2026 · CSTP 6A

What if I just wrote down what actually happened in class?

I noticed that my second class of the day always ran smoother than my first. I already knew why, sort of, but I was not capturing it in any organized way. So I started writing Post Class Thoughts within five minutes of each class ending, using speech-to-text to get it out fast before the moment passed.

The pattern that emerged pretty quickly: when I rushed through instructions, students struggled. When I slowed down and explained rather than just reading slides, things clicked. I caught myself stretching the beginning of a lesson and cramming the end, and I started putting a backup activity ready for when I had extra time instead of just letting the pacing fall apart. A Shark Tank group activity that ate up too much time across multiple sessions got redesigned for one focused day.

The student impact is hard to show in a single screenshot, but it is in every class that ran better the second time around because I had actually reflected on the first. This journal is already something I plan to use next year when I am teaching the same lessons again.
